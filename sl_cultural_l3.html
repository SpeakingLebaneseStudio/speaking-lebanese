Why Sunday Lunch Never Really Ends
Written by Nidale Bachir, from Beirut

The Lebanese family is not nuclear. It is not just two parents and two children in a flat. It is grandparents who live one floor below. It is the uncle who comes for lunch every Sunday without being invited, because he does not need to be invited. It is the aunt who knows your business before you have decided it yourself. And none of this is considered intrusion — it is considered love.

Every Lebanese family has a village. Not always a village they live in — most city families have not lived in their village for decades — but a village they belong to. You go there in August. You are buried there. Your grandfather's name is on a wall somewhere in that village, and people there will recognise your family name and know your history. For Lebanese diaspora who grew up in Sydney or Montreal or São Paulo, this connection can feel abstract. But when you go to Lebanon and someone in a small mountain village says "ah, min 3aylet... mna3réfon" — I know your family — you feel something shift in you.

Sunday lunch is the institution that holds the Lebanese family together. It starts around two in the afternoon and ends when it ends, which could be seven in the evening. There is no fixed guest list. People arrive. More chairs appear. The table extends. Someone calls from Paris and is put on speakerphone next to the kibbeh. The food is always too much. That is the point. Abundance at the table is how a Lebanese host shows love.

For diaspora families who do not have the grandparents nearby, the Sunday lunch becomes even more charged — it is the one ritual that everyone makes time for, the weekly reminder that the family is still a family despite the ocean between them and Lebanon.

In Lebanon, family is not who you live with — it is who you belong to.
