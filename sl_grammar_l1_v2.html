Grammar in Context

You don't need a verb to say who you are

In Lebanese, there is no word for "am," "is," or "are" when describing who someone is or where they're from. You simply leave it out.

This is called the zero-copula — and it's one of the first things that makes Lebanese feel very different from English. In English you say "I am from Beirut." In Lebanese you say "Ana min Beirut" — literally: "I from Beirut." The verb just isn't there, and that's exactly right.

The Rule
Pronoun + [thing you are] — no verb needed
Ana Justine = I am Justine

⚠️
Common mistake — introducing yourself

In English you say "I am called Sarah" — so learners often add ana before ismi. Don't. The word ismi already means "my name is" — the "my" is built in. Adding ana on top is redundant and unnatural.

✗ ana ismi Sarah — too much
✓ ismi Sarah — natural Lebanese
