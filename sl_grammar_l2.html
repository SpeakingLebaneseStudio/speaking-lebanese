Grammar in Context

Most job titles and adjectives take -e for the feminine

In Lebanese, changing a job title or adjective from masculine to feminine almost always means adding an ending — but which ending depends on the word.

Three patterns cover nearly everything you'll meet in this lesson: a regular pattern that just adds -e (heard as -et before another word), nationality words that add -iyyet, and one very common irregular word that breaks the rule entirely. Learn the pattern first — the exceptions are few, and you'll hear them enough to remember them naturally.

Rule 1 — The regular pattern
Add -e (heard as -et before another word)
6aaleb → 6aalbet
student (m) → student (f)

Most job titles in this lesson's vocabulary follow this pattern exactly:

مهندس / مهندسة
mhandess / mhandset
engineer (m/f)
معلم / معلمة
m3allem / m3allmet
teacher (m/f)
موظف / موظفة
mwazzaf / mwazzafet
employee (m/f)
خاطب / خاطبة
5aateb / 5aatbet
engaged (m/f)
طيب / طيبة
6ayyeb / 6ayybet
well-cooked / good (m/f)
Rule 2 — Nationality words
Add -iyyet
lebnéni → lebnéniyyet
Lebanese (m) → Lebanese (f)

Every nationality in this lesson's vocabulary takes this same ending:

امركاني / امركانية
amerkéni / amerkéniyyet
American (m/f)
فرنساوي / فرنساوية
franséwi / franséwiyyet
French (m/f)
انجليزي / انجليزية
ingleezi / ingliziyyet
British/English (m/f)
Rule 3 — Doctōr is irregular
Add -at, not -et
doctōr → doctōrat
doctor (m) → doctor (f)
⚠️
Common mistake — doctōret vs. doctōrat

Learners often default to -et for every word, including the irregulars. Doctōr is the one to watch in this lesson — the feminine is doctōrat, not "doctōret." The -et pattern is the most common, but not universal. When in doubt, listen for the recorded feminine form rather than guessing.

✗ hiyyé doctōret — not a real word
✓ hiyyé doctōrat — she is a doctor

One more thing worth knowing: 7akeem / 7akeemet is a colloquial word that also means "doctor" — and unlike doctōr, it's perfectly regular. So hiyyé doctōrat and hiyyé 7akeemet are both correct ways to say "she is a doctor." Same meaning, two different words, two different patterns.
