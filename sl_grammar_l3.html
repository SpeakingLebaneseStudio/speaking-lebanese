Grammar in Context

One ending turns "a house" into "my house"

Lebanese doesn't use separate words for "my," "your," "his," "her," "our," or "their" — it adds a small ending directly onto the noun itself.

Six endings cover every possessive form you'll use constantly with family words. Singular: -i for "my," -ak / -ik for "your" (said to a man or a woman), and -o / -a for "his" and "her." Plural: -na for "our," -kon for "your" (to a group), and -on for "their." Learn these six, and every family word from this lesson's vocabulary bank becomes personal — bayy (father) becomes bayyi (my father) or bayyna (our father) with nothing else in the sentence changing.

Rule 1 — "My"
Add -i
beyt → beyti
house → my house

Try it with words from this lesson's vocabulary bank:

جوزي
jowzi
my husband
إبني
ibni
my son
بنتي
binti
my daughter
Rule 2 — "Your"
Add -ak (to a man) or -ik (to a woman)
beyt → beytak / beytik
house → your house (m) / your house (f)

The same split you already know from Lesson 2's gendered -e — here it's about who you're talking to, not the noun itself:

عمَك / عمِك
3ammak / 3ammik
your uncle (to m/f)
عيلتَك / عيلتِك
3ayletak / 3ayletik
your family (to m/f)
Rule 3 — "His" and "Her"
Add -o for "his," -a for "her"
beyt → beyto / beyta
house → his house / her house

These two show up everywhere once you start talking about someone else's family:

جدو
jiddo
his grandfather
جوزا
jowza
her husband
Rule 4 — "Our"
Add -na
beyt → beytna
house → our house

This is the "ne7na" (we) ending — the same -na you'll hear attached all over the place once you're talking about your own household:

بيتنا
beytna
our house
عيلتنا
3ayletna
our family
بيّنا
bayyna
our father
Rule 5 — "Your" (to a group)
Add -kon
beyt → beytkon
house → your house (to more than one person)

This is the "you-all" ending — one form covers a whole group, no gender split like the singular "your" had:

بيتكن
beytkon
your house (to a group)
عيلتكن
3ayletkon
your family (to a group)
Rule 6 — "Their"
Add -on
beyt → beyton
house → their house

This is the "hinni" (they) ending — the last of the six, closing out the full set:

بيتن
beyton
their house
جدّن
jiddon
their grandfather
💡
"3ammo" isn't just grammar — it's what you call him

Look back at this lesson's vocabulary bank: jidd/jiddo, 3amm/3ammo, 3ammet/3amto, 5aal/5aalo, 5aalet/5aalto are exactly Rule 3 in action. The "-o" / "-to" form of a family word — grammatically "his grandfather," "his uncle" — is also what Lebanese speakers use as a direct, affectionate way to address that family member, even their own. A kid doesn't call out "3amm!" to their uncle — they call out "3ammo!"

✓ jiddo 3am biyeekol — his grandfather is eating (Rule 3)
✓ Jiddo, ta3! (calling your own grandpa) — Grandpa, come here! (direct address)

Same word, two jobs. Context tells you which one you're hearing — if there's no other family member being discussed, it's almost always the direct-address sense.
