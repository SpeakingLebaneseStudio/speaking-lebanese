Grammar in Context

Point at what you want — Lebanese does the rest

Ordering barely needs full sentences. Pair a demonstrative — "this" or "that" — with baddi ("I want") or 36eeni ("give me"), and you can ask for almost anything on the table.

Lebanese demonstratives change shape for gender and number, the same way you've already seen adjectives do since Lesson 2's gendered -e. There's a near set for "this/these" and a far set for "that/those" — six forms in total, three per set. Then two small request phrases, baddi and 36eeni, turn any noun from this lesson's vocabulary bank into something you can actually order.

Rule 1 — "This / These" (near)
haida (m) · haydi (f) · holé (pl)
ma63am → haida ma63am
restaurant → this restaurant

The form has to match the noun's gender — the same masculine/feminine split you already track from Lesson 2's job titles:

هيدا مطعم
haida ma63am
this restaurant (m)
هيدي طاولة
haydi 6aawlet
this table (f)
هول مناقيش
holé mné2eesh
these manakeesh (pl)
Rule 2 — "That / Those" (far)
heydaak (m) · heydeek (f) · hõleek (pl)
ma63am → heydaak ma63am
restaurant → that restaurant

Same three nouns, same gender agreement — only the near/far distance changes:

هَيداك مطعم
heydaak ma63am
that restaurant (m)
هَيديك طاولة
heydeek 6aawlet
that table (f)
هوليك مناقيش
hõleek mné2eesh
those manakeesh (pl)
Rule 3 — "I want..."
baddi + noun
3a9eer → baddi 3a9eer
juice → I want juice

Baddi doesn't change for gender or number — it stays exactly the same no matter what you're asking for:

بدي ميّ
baddi mayy
I want water
بدي عصير
baddi 3a9eer
I want juice
Rule 4 — "Give me..."
36eeni + noun
7ommo9 → 36eeni 7ommo9
hummus → give me hummus

More direct than baddi — useful once you're actually pointing at what you want:

عطيني حمص
36eeni 7ommo9
give me hummus
عطيني كبة
36eeni kibbé
give me kibbeh
💡
Put the two rules together — you don't even need the noun

In real ordering, Lebanese speakers often skip the noun entirely and just point: baddi haida — "I want this one" — works for anything on a menu or a table, no vocabulary required. The demonstrative alone (haida/haydi/heydaak/heydeek) already answers "which one?" so baddi + demonstrative is often shorter and more natural than baddi + the full noun.

✓ baddi haida — I want this one (pointing, no noun needed)
✓ baddi 3a9eer — I want juice (naming the noun instead)

Both are correct — which one you use just depends on whether you're naming the thing or pointing at it.
